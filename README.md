## Abstract

<small>
Highly pathogenic avian influenza (HPAI) H5Nx clade 2.3.4.4b has been circulating in Europe since 2016, posing a threat to poultry, wildlife, and public health. Despite the ongoing economic and pandemic threat, the determinants of viral spillover, spread, and annual emergence remain poorly understood. To address this gap, we integrated genomic, ecological, and epidemiological data from 2016 to 2025 into a comparative genetic framework, allowing us to quantify viral spread among regions, habitats, and hosts.  We analyzed 7,031 European H5Nx HA sequences, linked host and habitat traits from AVONET, defined five geographic “Regions” using k-means clustering, and generated three balanced subsamples of 1,118 sequences each for phylogeographic analysis. Our analyses indicated that transmission hubs have shifted over time, with Central-Western Europe serving as a persistent reservoir and an early major exporter and persisting through 2021-2023, while Central-Southern Europe became increasingly important from 2021 onward. Wetlands formed the backbone of transmission, maintaining long-term circulation before repeated jumps into farms, grasslands, and coastal areas. The interface between wetlands and farms was the key driver of the spread.  Anatidae migration and live-poultry trade were important predictors for inter-regional transmission. Together, these results show that European H5Nx dynamics are structured by regional transmission hubs that shift over time, as well as persistent wetland reservoirs, highlighting waterfowl migratory connectivity and trade corridors as priorities for targeted surveillance and control. 
</small>
